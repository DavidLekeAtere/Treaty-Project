Modern Day Context of Treaty 10

Study how treaty 10 affects the modern land and people involved in Treaty 10

The modern context of Treaty 10 is shaped by ongoing discussions about land rights, resource management, and the relationship between Indigenous peoples and the Canadian government. As society evolves, so too does the interpretation and implementation of treaties.



Creating Urban Reserves via Treaty Land Entitlements Under the original treaty, the federal government miscalculated population sizes, resulting in First Nations receiving much less reserve land than they were legally owed. For decades, leaders campaigned to correct these discrepancies. Through the Treaty Land Entitlement (TLE) Framework Agreement, Treaty 10 nations like English River fought for and won the right to buy land on the open market to expand their borders. Instead of accepting remote northern plots, they strategically bought land near major economic hubs. They established a highly successful urban reserve south of Saskatoon, using their treaty status to build commercial businesses, generate independent revenue, and create jobs for their members.

What Makes Treaty 10 Different?

1. Lands in Severalty

Treaty 10 allowed individual Indigenous families to claim 160 acres of private land outside a shared reserve. This was different from earlier treaties that grouped entire communities onto one reserve.

This system was created because many northern Cree and Chipewyan peoples lived in small, mobile hunting groups spread across large areas rather than in permanent villages.

2. Created Because of New Provinces

Earlier numbered treaties were mainly made to open land for farming settlers and railway construction.

Treaty 10 was delayed for many years, even after Indigenous communities requested help during times of starvation. It was finally rushed through in 1906 after Saskatchewan and Alberta became provinces in 1905, which pushed the government to take control of northern lands.

3. Focus on Natural Resources

Unlike southern treaty areas that were suited for farming, the northern lands in Treaty 10 were not ideal for agriculture.

The Canadian government wanted access to valuable resources such as timber and minerals found in the north.

4. Unusual Boundaries

Treaty 10 covered about 220,000 square kilometres across northern Saskatchewan and a small part of eastern Alberta.

Even though part of Alberta was included, no Alberta First Nations signed the treaty. Instead, two First Nations from Manitoba signed it even though their communities were outside the official treaty area.

5. Métis Scrip Happened at the Same Time

During the Treaty 10 negotiations, the government also sent a Scrip Commissioner to deal with Métis land claims.

Métis people were offered scrip that could be exchanged for either 240 acres of land or $240 in cash.
